Diagnostic Tests

Used for persons in whom disease is suspected, due to symptoms, physical findings, or laboratory findings.

    Diagnostic Tests can be:
  • Invasive,
  • dangerous,
  • expensive,
  • not feasible on large groups of people,
  • time consuming
Versus

Screening Tests

used for identifying early signs of a disease in people with no signs or symptoms of the disease.

    usually separates an asymptomatic population into:
  • those who almost certainly do not have the disease and
  • those who may have it.

Follow screen with a diagnostic workup to determine whether those who screened positive in fact have the disease before treatment is started.

Sensitivity

Sensitivity is the proportion of persons with disease who test positive using the screening test. It determines the test's ability to correctly identify individuals who truly have the disease. In other words, it is the proportion or probability that a person will test positive if they truly have the disease.

Specificity

Specificity is the proportion of persons without the disease (true negatives) who test negative. It helps determine the test's ability to correctly identify individuals who truly do not have disease. It gives us the probability the test will be negative if a subject truly does not have the disease.

Predictive Value Positive (PVP)

Predictive value positive is the predictive value of a positive test. It gives us the proportion of persons testing positive who truly have the disease. This is also described as the test 'yield.' It is used to answer the question, "now that a person has tested positive, what is the probability that he or she truly has the disease?"

Predictive Value Negative (PVN)

Predictive value negative is the predictive value of a negative test. It gives us the proportion of persons testing negative who truly do not have the disease.

Instructions

Validity is a measure of accuracy in relation to the truth. A special 4-fold table is used to evaluate the indices of validity. The screening test results, positive or negative, are displayed along the left side (rows) and the person's "true" disease status based on the diagnostic gold-standard test is indicated at the top (columns). The people who correctly screen positive or correctly screen negative are shown in cells A and D. The people who falsely screen positive or falsely screen negative are shown in cells B and C. We use this table to evaluate the clinical usefulness of a test by comparing the results of the screening test under evaluation with those derived from a definitive diagnostic procedure ("gold standard").

  1. Ideal tests are both valid and reliable.
  2. Tests with high validity but low reliability are useful for measuring group values but not for measuring values for a given individual.
  3. Tests with high reliability but low validity are repeatable for a given individual but the resulting value is not predictive of the intended value.
  4. Tests with both low validity and low reliability are essentially useless.
